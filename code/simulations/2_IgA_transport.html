Objective

In this document, we develop a model of IgA transport across mucosal epithelium. Our goal is to understand the extent to which pre-existing IgA antibodies to historical infections limits the function newly generated responses, and we hypothesize that this constraint comes mainly from competition for pIgR transport proteins.

Simulation Outline

We will assume a constant rate of 1) response generation and decay in the serum and subsequent diffusion into the tissue; 2) response generation and decay in the mucosal tissue; and 3) a constant rate of IgA transport out of the epithelium at steady state.

We will run these conditions to steady-state, such that the concentration of IgA in the mucosal tissue is constant.

At equilibrium, we will begin a new response, such that the antibody titers in the serum increase and decay, the titers in the mucosa increase and decay, the rate of diffusion into the epithelium increases and decays, and the number of transport proteins increases and decays. We will look at the dynamics of the flyx of antibodies into the lumen. We are interested in: how much gets over and how early?

Model Description

We will consider two populations competing for transport: Newly generated IgA specific for the current infection, \(M_s\), and IgA in the mucosa remaining from prior infections, \(M_{ns}\).

We will model competition as an enzymatic reaction facilitated by polymeric IgA receptors, pIgRs.

Competition

We model competition for transport proteins as a Michaelis-Menten enzymatic model with direct competition. This gives the following equation for the flux of the specific antibody into the lumen per unit time:

\[ \Phi_{L,s}(t) = \frac{V_{max}(t)}{K + M_{ns}(t) + M_s(t)} M_s(t) \] Notice that 1) \(K\) should be the same for both antibodies as there should be no difference in affinity between the two for pIgR, and thus we would expect competition to be symmetric; and 2) \(V_{max}(t)\) is a function of time, representing the increase in pIgR expression and deposition at the basolateral surface of the epithelial cells over the course of the initial response to infection. \(M_{ns}(t)\) and \(M_s(t)\) represent the local concentrations of the nonspecific and specific antibodies in the mucosa at the basolateral side of epithelial cells. These quantities change over the course of infection

The core of the competition comes from the relationship between the antibody concentrations in the mucosa, \(M_{ns}\) and \(M_s\), and \(K\).

We define this equation in the code chunk below:

Pre-existing, non-sepecific antibody titer

One factor affecting the extent to which newly-generate IgA is inhibited by competition is on the steady-state concentration of nonspecific antibody in the mucosa.

This concentration is dependent on 1) the rate of infections, 2) the the production, decay, and diffusion of systemic IgA during one of those infections, and 3) the contribution of local production and subsequent decay of IgA during those infections.

We have some sense of the steady state concentration of IgA in serum; what we can vary is the steady state concentration of IgA in the mucosal tissue.

In this document, I will simplify this situation such that we only consider dynamics in the mucosal compartment. As such, I make the following asusmptions about the composition of IgA in the mucosal compartment:

  1. IgA flows into the mucosal compartment (due to local production and flux from the blood) at a constant rate, \(\gamma_m\);
  2. IgA flows out of the mucosal compartment due to i) decay exponentially in the mucosal compartment with rate \(d_\mu\), and ii) transport out of the mucosal compartment at a rate \(\frac{V_{max,0}}{K + M_{ns}(t)}\).

Note that \(\gamma_m\) is composed of two quantities: 1) the mean production of IgA locally, which comes from the average of the sum of exponentially decaying antibody responses \(A_0e^{-d_\mu t}\) at a constant rate of \(1/\tau\) and takes the form \(A_0/\tau\), where \(\tau\) is the time between responses, and 2) a constant rate of diffusion of serum IgA into the mucosa, which we can call \(f\). Thus, the concentration of IgA in the mucosal tissue changes as:

\[ \frac{dM_{ns}}{dt} = \gamma_m - d_\mu M_{ns} - \frac{V_{max,0}}{K+M_{ns}}M_{ns} \\ = \frac{A_0}{\tau} + f - d_\mu M_{ns} - \frac{V_{max,0}}{K+M_{ns}}M_{ns} \]

Later, we can change \(f\) as a function of time, meaning that the rate of diffusion changes over the course of the response. For now, however, we keep this as a constant.

The steady-state concentration of non-specific mucosal IgA in this system can be solved for as follows:

$$ 0 = m - dM_{ns} - M_{ns} \ = m(K+M{ns}) - d_(K+M_{ns})M_{ns} - V_{max,0}M_{ns} \ = m K + m M{ns} - dK M_{ns} - d_M_{ns}^2 - V_{max,0}M_{ns} \ = d_M_{ns}^2 + (V_{max,0} - m + dK)M_{ns} - _m K

$$ with solution

\[ M_{ns}^* = \frac{(\gamma_m -V_{max,0} - d_\mu K) + \sqrt{(V_{max,0} - \gamma_m + d_\mu K)^2 + 4d_\mu \gamma_m K}}{2d_\mu} \] as we will want the positive root and \(\gamma_m\), \(K\), and \(d_\mu\) are all greater than 0.

I define the equation for this steady state concewntration of \(M_{ns}\) in the code chunk below:

Newly generated specific antibodies

A new response can either be generated “systemically” – that is, generated in a secondary lymphoid organ and diffuse into the mucosal tissue – or generated in the mucosal tissue directly.

The Brochal Associated Lymphatic Tissue (BALT) provides a fast local lymphatic response, where newly generated plasma cells immediately diffuse into the mucosa. I’m not sure whether the diffusion here should be a barrier, but for now I will not model it that way

As such, we will generate the new response locally, according to a function that exponentially increases, then decreases, the specific antibody titer. The increase will rise to a peak \(A_0\) and decay at rate \(d_\mu\), the same parameters we used to estimate the steady state concentration of non-specific antibodies generated in the mucosa. Duh!

This function – from Andreas and murphy – takes the form:

\[ A(t) = \frac{A_0}{1+e^{(-r(t-\rho))}}e^{-d_\mu t} \]

Where \(r\) is the initial growth rate in antibody titers, \(\rho\) is the time of the peak of the response, \(A_0\) is the peak of the response, and \(d_\mu\) is the exponential decay rate of antibodies in the mucosa.

This function assumes antibodies increase exponentially at a rate \(r\) and decrease exponentially at a rate \(d_\mu\).

I define this function in the code chunk below:

# Concentration of mucosal specific IgA during new response
M_specific = function(A0, r, rho, d_mu) {(A0*exp(-d_mu*t))/(1+exp(-r*(t-rho)))}
